# Fourth Meridian — Staff Architecture Review

**Date:** 2026-07-16
**Posture:** Investment-grade critical review. Code-verified against the working tree as of today; every material claim below was checked against source, schema, or the project's own audit corpus. Where I cite a file, I read it.
**Scope reviewed:** `lib/` semantic layer (~40 core modules), `prisma/schema.prisma` (2,772 lines, 50 models), `components/dashboard/SpaceDashboard.tsx` and the space/shell surface, `app/api/ai/chat/route.ts` and the AI pipeline, jobs/events/notifications/policy layers, and the doctrine corpus (FI0, Financial Semantic Authorities, Space Contract Doctrine, Phase 2 docs, STATUS.md, both July audits).

---

## Executive summary

Fourth Meridian is a top-decile engineering effort with a bottom-decile activation record, run at a process weight the project itself admits it cannot sustain. The semantic core — FlowType, transfer evidence, money/FX, the perspective engine, the investments event log — is genuinely good work that most funded fintech startups do not have. But the codebase currently tells three different stories about the same dollar on the same date, stores every one of those dollars as a `Float`, renders its flagship surface from a 3,725-line god component whose governing doctrine was ratified *today* with zero of eight phases built, and runs on a platform substrate (single daily cron hitting an API route, synchronous in-process "events," no queue, no observability) that hits a wall several orders of magnitude before "millions of users."

If I were deciding whether to invest millions into this codebase: I would invest in the semantic layer and the person who built it, and I would write off roughly a third of the surrounding structure as ceremony that must be deleted or activated before it hardens into permanent debt. The binding constraint is not architecture. It is proportion — the gap between what has been built and what any user can see, verify, or pay for.

## 1. Overall architecture

**What is impressive.** The write/read authority discipline in the transaction layer is real, not aspirational: one versioned classifier writes flow semantics (`flow-classifier.ts`, `FLOW_CLASSIFIER_VERSION = 2`), one pure zero-import predicate module reads them (`flow-predicates.ts`), and the fold discipline in `cash-flow.ts` (`foldEconomicRow` as the only three-way branch) is enforced by structure rather than convention. The money layer is the best subsystem in the repo: a single `convertMoney`, append-only USD-cross FX archive with deterministic walk-back, convert-then-sum with taint propagation, and honest degradation to native-plus-`estimated` on rate misses. The investments side got an actual event log (`InvestmentEvent` → anchored backward reconstruction → `PositionObservation`) with shared valuation cores so current and historical views agree by construction. Provenance doctrine — versioned backfills, additive migrations, evidence axes, `UNKNOWN` as an honest classifier output — is applied consistently. This is the kind of foundation that products like Monarch and Copilot conspicuously lack under their category engines.

**What is overengineered.** Four things stand out. First, the doctrine corpus itself: a 212KB STATUS.md that doubled in eight days, ~440 docs, a frozen initiative namespace with an alias table to resolve ID collisions, and adversarial closeout audits — for a solo-founder, pre-user, Vercel-Hobby-tier deployment. The project's own audits say this three times ("documentation weight exceeding maintenance capacity"). Second, speculative machinery built ahead of its second consumer, against FI0 §12's own rule: `TransferRail` is a one-member union with a full axis ontology and seven provenance columns per row; the authority-tier system in `transfer-evidence-write.ts` has zero writers above the provider tier; `lib/space-templates/` is a byte-identical duplicate of `space-presets.ts` with a parity test proving it duplicates ("nothing in the app reads this registry yet"). Third, the widget metadata layer: 1,193 lines in `widget-registry.ts` for 31 metadata-only entries whose `dataTier` field is dead — actual fetches are gated by hand-coded booleans in the host. Fourth, persisted TI2 facts that trivially restate row columns (`settlementState = pending ? PENDING : POSTED`), which FI0 §16 itself names as an anti-pattern.

**What is underengineered.** Money as `Float` — 59 columns, including `Transaction.amount`, every `SpaceSnapshot` total, and `FxRate.rate`, with zero `Decimal` anywhere and no money library in `package.json`. The system compensates with at least five different epsilons scattered across domains (0.005 in `RelationshipResolver`, 1e-6 in reconstruction, 1e-9 in the doctrine oracle, 0.5 in wealth). This is ratified debt ("Float confirmed — plan D3") compounding through FX multiplication and multi-year walk-backs into the product's headline number. Second: no double-entry, no persisted transfer pair (`transferGroupId` deferred since the FlowType foundation doc), which is why the debt A/B/C views "will not reconcile exactly" by design and cross-currency internal transfers are structurally unresolvable in an explicitly multi-currency product. Third: the entire operational floor — no Sentry, no APM, no alerting, no queue, no backup restore drill, CSP still Report-Only.

**Enterprise for enterprise's sake.** The initiative bureaucracy (TI0–TI5, KD-1–22, A-track with a pending renumbering "deferred to founder approval," retroactively allocated track IDs for work that shipped unrecorded); the platform-grant RBAC axis and four Platform Spaces with fourteen ops widgets serving an audience of one; source-scan regex tests that pin comments as much as code (`space-shell-seams.test.ts` asserting `!/renderHero/.test(dashCode)`); golden byte-identity serialization gates before any external consumer exists.

**Where I would simplify.** Delete or wire `space-templates` (a parity-tested duplicate is pure carrying cost). Collapse the three goals caches and the Personal server/client double-fetch into one data layer. Merge `SectionRegistry` (48 renderer entries inside the god component) into `widget-registry` (31 metadata entries) before any new workspace is added. Freeze the doc process to STATUS + per-initiative one-pagers. And stop building second-provider abstractions until a second provider exists.

## 2. Semantic layer

**Is it actually clean?** About 80% yes, and the remaining 20% is exactly where drift is already happening. The single-authority claim holds for the classifier/predicate pair, but flow semantics are in truth a *two-stage* pipeline: the legacy `mapPlaidCategory` in `syncTransactions.ts` still sits upstream, and `classifyFlow`'s precedence (PFC → `category` → sign) consumes that mapper's lossy output — the exact "semantic table hidden in a parser" anti-pattern FI0 §7 names. Worse for the doctrine's credibility: `plaid-transfer-evidence.ts` declares itself "the ONLY module that knows Plaid's detailed transfer strings," and that claim is false — `flow-classifier.ts:173` string-matches `ACCOUNT_TRANSFER` for the CF-4 override. A Plaid taxonomy change now has two blast sites and a doctrine that says it has one.

**Hidden duplications.** The coarse income/expense/transfer/debt partition is stated three times (predicates, `BANKING_POPULATION` Prisma fragment, AI assembler), reconciled only by a population test. `legacyBucket()`/`classifierBucket()` in `plaid-flow-input.ts` is a live third restatement labeled "shadow observability." `SPEND_CATEGORIES` hand-mirrors the Prisma enum with no compile guard. The account-name resolution convention (`displayName ?? officialName ?? plaidName ?? name`) exists by copy-paste in at least three files. `isReconstructableCard` is an admitted verbatim copy between `backfill.ts` and `regenerate-history.ts` because a `server-only` import chain made sharing awkward — a module-boundary problem solved by duplicating a *financial predicate*. None of these is fatal; all of them are the seeds of the next "why do these two numbers differ" investigation.

**Unnecessary abstraction.** Covered above (transfer authority tiers, TI2 trivial facts, template registry). One addition: every flow predicate takes `Flow = string | null | undefined` — deliberately stringly-typed at every read site, so `isTransfer('TRANSFERR')` compiles and silently returns false. The most load-bearing enum in the system is protected by compile-checked maps on the write path and by nothing on the read path.

**Does the doctrine make sense? Would another architect understand it?** The doctrine documents are unusually candid — the Space Contract Doctrine's Implemented/Planned split is accurate in every case I checked, including admitting its own flagship loader is unwired. Another architect *would* understand the intent. What they could not do is trust the prose: multiple doctrine claims are already false in code (transfer-string ownership, "No Prisma" headers — `reconstruction-core.ts` value-imports `InvestmentEventType` from `@prisma/client` while its header claims purity — schema formula comments that predate `realAssets`, a README that still describes a local-first Docker product). This codebase enforces architecture through prose more than through types, and the prose has a measurable error rate. That is the semantic layer's deepest weakness: its invariants live in comments, custom test harnesses (`npx tsx`, outside any standard runner), and 255 oracle checks that are only as green as the discipline to run them.

## 3. Space architecture

This is the weakest area of the codebase, and the doctrine's honesty does not change the runtime facts.

**SpaceShell / WorkspaceDefinition.** Neither exists as a code symbol anywhere in `components/`, `app/`, or `lib/`. The Space Contract Doctrine was ratified *today* (2026-07-16); its §15 is an eight-phase decomposition plan with zero phases built. What exists at runtime is `SpaceDashboard.tsx`: 3,725 lines, 13 props, 33 `useState`, 19 `useEffect`, 19 `fetch()` call sites against ~12 endpoints, an in-file zoo of ~11 components including a ~430-line `GoalsCard` with its own CRUD, in-file financial math (`projectFV`, emergency-fund coverage) in direct violation of the host-never-computes rule, and a hand-rolled invalidation bus made of two nonce counters, window `CustomEvent`s, and a 12-second `setInterval`. The actual composition model is a hardcoded seven-branch if/else ladder on `activePerspectiveId`; the five flagship workspaces bypass the registry entirely. The `investments` perspective declares a widget key that exists in no registry and is consumed only for `.length > 0` truthiness. This is not an architecture with a hotspot; it is a monolith with a plan.

**SpaceData contracts.** Two exist (`investments/space-data.ts`, `connections/space-data.ts`) and they do not share a type, an envelope shape, or even a scope axis (one is Space-scoped, the other user-scoped). Four more are "Planned." A doctrine generalized from n=2, where the flagship instance — `loadInvestmentsSpaceData`, composed, fixture-tested — has **zero consumers**; the UI reads a different path through `useInvestmentsTimeMachine`. Calling this a contract system is premature; it is two loaders and a memo.

**Loader ownership.** Split and contested. The Personal page server-fetches accounts/snapshots/transactions, then `SpaceDashboard` client-refetches the same three resources into its own state. Goals are fetched by three uncoordinated owners (GoalsCard, the workspace effect, ManageSpaceModal's tab), kept consistent by window events. The full space object is fetched solely to display a member count that two other components fetch again. There is no query cache, no SWR/react-query, no RSC caching strategy — a "Part-2 fix" comment documents `router.refresh()` failing to reach client effects, i.e., the two data planes are already fighting. The "canonical loader doctrine" is enforced by a regex source-scan test.

**URL ownership.** Four independent `window.history` writers with four `popstate` listeners: the tab/perspective effect, the `?metric` writer (which the doctrine's own inventory fails to count), `usePerspectiveShellState` (`?asof`/`?compareto`/`?preset`), and `DashboardChrome` (`?transaction`). Writes are unserialized, so one gesture can push two history entries and Back traverses half-states. The doctrine correctly names this the highest-priority coupling (Phase 0A); it is unfixed, and `page.tsx` still carries a comment claiming the dashboard "has no URL sync" — false since the URL slice landed.

**FX ownership.** One good display-currency context, then a switchboard: the host reconciles `widgetMoneyCtx`, `spaceMoneyCtx`, `transactionsMoneyCtxOverride`, `perspectiveTargetCurrency`, and `snapshotCurrency` by hand, with the fallback expression recomputed at three call sites and currency changes propagated by a window event plus nonce plus per-cache clears.

**Time ownership.** The `time-range.ts` reducer is the best code in the UI layer — pure, invariant-preserving, round-trip tested. But it is one of three time states: `cashFlowPeriod` is hand-bridged with a documented desync mode, `chartMetric` is a third URL-synced view state, and investments deliberately runs two time axes ("so the two time axes never collide" — an admission that two exist). Liquidity and Debt silently ignore the shell's As-Of with nothing telling the user the date picker is inert.

**Verdict.** Is this a genuinely scalable UI architecture? No — not yet, and not as built. The shared *service* layer (perspective engine, time reducer, conversion contexts) is real engineering. The UI layer is invented patterns documented after the fact: adding a workspace today touches at least six places (ladder branch, fetch boolean, host state, envelope resolver, routing maps, registry), a third-party widget is impossible (renderers are closures over host state; the registry stores no component references), and the platform surface — ironically the cleanest implementation of the registry idea, with self-fetching widgets via `widget-kit.tsx` — proves the pattern works while the customer surface never got it. The doctrine is a good plan. A plan is not an architecture.

## 4. Financial model

**FlowType** is a sound canonical model and the strongest single decision in the codebase — a closed enum with an honest `UNKNOWN`, versioned classification, and selective backfill. Its weaknesses are the upstream legacy category stage and the stringly-typed read surface, both covered above.

**Transfer evidence** is conceptually coherent and well-layered: adapter → persistence policy → pure matcher → impure orchestrator is a clean split, and persisting evidence axes rather than resolved pairs (resolution at read time, self-healing on relink) is a defensible trade. Three real problems: the matcher requires exact currency and half-cent amount equality, so cross-currency transfers and fee-bearing wires are *permanently* unresolvable and silently inflate `unresolved`; the same row's disposition varies by viewing Space (KD-15 visibility gating), meaning "the liquidity effect of a row" is a (row, viewer) fact — privacy-correct but stated plainly nowhere; and a persisted `counterpartyAccountId` coexists with read-time resolution under a precedence rule that lives in a comment.

**RelationshipResolver** is pure and disciplined (refuses ambiguity rather than guessing), but its canonical output reaches the cash-flow fold as an untyped cast — `(t as { transferDisposition?: ... })` — so the compiler cannot see whether callers supply it.

**Snapshots / Time Machine / historical reconstruction.** Here the model fails its own standard. There are **six distinct history mechanisms** (live regenerate, 30-day backfill, A9 windowed regeneration, amendment rewrites, investments reconstruction, and two time machines — investments and wealth) that only partially share cores, and they demonstrably disagree: the wealth series values investments at provider balance for today but at quantity×price×FX for regenerated history (a structural kink at the today/yesterday boundary); A10 values `detailEligible` scope while snapshots keep `all`, so a shared Space's wealth and investments surfaces answer "what were my investments worth on date D" differently by design; frozen observed rows never re-agree after price backfills; and as-of semantics are three different rules (nearest-≤ with no staleness ceiling, strict-as-of with surfaced staleness, latest-observation with none) plus a local-time/UTC split in period math (`cash-flow.ts` uses local `getFullYear` while everything else is UTC). `realAssets` is not even a snapshot column — it is back-derived as a clamped residual, in a codebase whose doctrine bans derived residuals presented as composition. The project's own 07-12 audit found the same class of defect (C1–C4: no invalidation chain from fact changes to persisted history, completeness tier flattened at the snapshot layer, silent history rewrites on unlink). The cross-perspective investigation of 07-11 documented these inconsistencies; A1–A10 then shipped and the inconsistency *migrated* rather than closed.

**Financial facts** (`transaction-facts.ts`) are honestly a versioned, persisted cache of derived values, not an independent fact layer — which is fine, except that facts derived *from* flow columns (`paymentMethod`) have no cross-invalidation when flow reclassification runs: two version lattices, no joint coherence. Truth ownership is otherwise clear (raw rows + captured provider metadata are substrate; facts re-derive).

**Could another company build on this?** The pure cores — classifier, predicates, evidence derivation, matcher, folds, money/convert, valuation, time-machine cores — could be extracted into a standalone engine in days; they are DB-free and mostly type-only in their Prisma imports. That is a real asset. But the *identity and visibility* layer (population rules relying on Prisma null semantics, `SpaceAccountLink` visibility threaded into liquidity semantics, KD-15 gating inside transfer resolution) is the app, and it cannot be extracted. And no company builds on Float money with per-domain epsilons. Verdict: the math is portable; the platform is not; the substrate disqualifies both until Decimal lands.

## 5. Technical debt (architectural, 2-year horizon)

Ranked by expected pain in 2028:

1. **Float money.** Every year of history, every FX multiplication, every walk-back compounds binary error into net worth. Migrating 59 columns across 50 models *after* accumulating years of snapshots and amendment records is an order of magnitude harder than now. This is the single most expensive-to-defer item in the repo, and it is ratified ("plan D3", "execute post-v2.6b").
2. **Six history mechanisms with no reconciliation authority.** Every future feature that touches the past (amendments, backfills, corrections, new asset classes) multiplies against six code paths and three as-of semantics. In two years this is a permanent "numbers team" tax. The missing abstraction is one as-of resolution authority and one invalidation chain (fact change → dependent history), which the 07-12 audit already demanded (C1).
3. **Five concurrent dual-model migration seams** (`PlaidItem`↔`Connection`, provider identity, `Holding`↔observations, flat debt↔`DebtProfile`, dormant enums) — the DATABASE_ARCHITECTURE_REVIEW's own "permanently dual-tracked" failure mode. Legacy `Account` took months and an admitted inventory undercount to retire; each open seam ages the same way. Crypto sync still writes *only* legacy `Holding`.
4. **The god components.** `SpaceDashboard.tsx` (3,725), `ManageSpaceModal.tsx` (1,742 — seven inlined sub-applications duplicating goals/sections/accounts CRUD), `chat/route.ts` (2,199), `annotations.ts` (2,184). Every initiative queues on the first; the project's audit says exactly this. Two years of feature work lands *inside* these files unless Phase 0A/0B happen first.
5. **Prose-enforced invariants.** Schema comments that are wrong ("nothing writes or reads them yet" on columns filtered in production), doctrine ownership claims falsified by code, regex source-scan tests, a custom test runner outside any ecosystem. As the surface grows, the error rate of the prose grows linearly and the trust in it decays faster.
6. **Activation debt as architecture.** The 07-12 audit's "dark system" finding: A-series capabilities behind env flags that appear in no env file, `asOf` dying at the HTTP boundary, `cash-flow-compare.ts` with zero consumers, A9 with no trigger wiring at audit time. Dark code is not neutral — it rots against a moving schema, and it already required retroactive initiative IDs to even account for it.
7. **Session/auth line.** NextAuth v4 on Next 16 with an acknowledged JWT-revocation gap ("full revocation requires a token blocklist") and one shared `ENCRYPTION_KEY` for Plaid tokens, TOTP, and DOB with no rotation story. For a financial product this graduates from debt to liability the day real users arrive.
8. **No validation layer at the API boundary.** Hand-parsed bodies, `category as never` casts that bypass enum checking entirely. Every new route re-earns the same injection/coercion risks.

## 6. Missing platform pieces

Things that genuinely belong and are absent, in priority order:

- **A queue / background-work substrate.** This is the most structural absence. The cron→route→loop-over-everything design cannot fan out, cannot retry, and caps every job at one serverless invocation's duration. Snapshot regeneration, sync, price backfills, and AI precomputation all need it. Nothing else on this list can be fixed properly without it.
- **Observability.** No Sentry, no APM, no alerting, no uptime monitor. Failures are discovered by looking at a dashboard widget. The hand-rolled substitutes (JobRun ledger, SyncIssue, ops widgets) are good *inputs* to observability, not replacements.
- **A real event outbox.** `lib/events/emit.ts` is honest that it is a synchronous in-process dispatcher, but handler failure = `console.warn` and silently lost side effects (a failed snapshot-regen handler leaves a stale net-worth chart with no reconciliation). The two-phase persist/dispatch design is one table away from an outbox; build it.
- **Feature flags with targeting.** Global boolean env vars gate everything, which is why the dark-system problem exists: activation is deploy-time, all-or-nothing, and currently *impossible by configuration* for six flags. Percentage/user-level flags are also the missing bridge between "founder-only beta" and real cohorts.
- **Product analytics.** The July-7 audit said it plainly: enormous investment in how to be correct, near-zero evidence of demand validation. No analytics SDK exists. For a product thesis this ambitious, this is the most dangerous absence on the list, and it is not an engineering-hard problem.
- **Search.** No infra, no tsvector/trgm, nothing. For a transactions product this is a first-class user expectation, and the semantic layer (merchants, flows, facts) would make it unusually good — it is a wasted advantage today.
- **Conversation persistence / memory for AI.** No `conversationId` anywhere; full history re-POSTed per turn; no cached assembled context. This is both a cost problem and the missing substrate for "durable knowledge."
- Rate limiting, audit trail, and notifications *exist* and are respectable — they do not belong on this list. A plugin architecture and workflow engine do **not** belong yet; the registry seams (assemblers, widgets, notifications) are the right pre-plugin posture, and building more would be more speculative machinery.

## 7. Product strategy

The vision: *compute financial understanding once, persist it as durable knowledge, reuse it everywhere, let AI reason over semantic facts rather than raw transactions.*

The codebase is heading there in its bones and drifting from it in its behavior. Persisted flow semantics, transfer evidence, merchant resolution, investment events, and daily snapshots genuinely are "compute once, persist" — the AI assemblers filter on persisted `flowType`, not raw Plaid strings, which is precisely the vision. But the last mile inverts it: every chat message re-fetches up to 5,000 rows per Space, re-runs read-time transfer resolution, re-aggregates, re-computes the assessment — for every eligible Space in parallel, unbounded, in master mode — with nothing cached between two messages of the same conversation. The time machines compute at read. The perspectives batch route recomputes every lens on every dashboard load with no caching (an open, self-logged issue). So the true state is: *facts* are computed once; *understanding* is recomputed on every request. The vision's second half — durable knowledge, reused everywhere — has no substrate: no conversation memory, no materialized per-space aggregates (despite `SpaceSnapshot` proving the pattern in-house), no insight persistence (`AiAdvice` has never had a write path; the ambient scheduler entrypoint "is never invoked" — the July-7 audit's sharpest finding: *the single feature the entire vision rests on is not executable today*).

There is also a quieter drift: the ontology was designed for an ambient, multi-domain life-operating-system (domain manifest enumerates PROPERTY, VEHICLE, TRIP, EQUIPMENT — all falling back to FINANCE_CORE; `templateId` accepted and ignored), while all shipped value is a personal-finance dashboard. The architecture is loyal to the vision; the shipping behavior is loyal to a much smaller product. Two years of that gap is how category-defining visions quietly become well-built Mint clones.

## 8. Competitive analysis

Compared against Monarch, Copilot Money, Quicken, Origin, and Kubera — all of which now ship some form of AI assistant or "intelligence" layer per their current positioning ([Origin's comparisons](https://useorigin.com/resources/blog/copilot-vs-monarch-vs-origin-which-personal-finance-app-is-actually-worth-it), [feature roundups](https://x1wealth.com/compare/copilot-vs-monarch), [2026 AI-tool surveys](https://www.techno-pulse.com/2026/04/best-ai-personal-finance-tools-in-2026.html)):

**Advantages actually built.** (1) A persisted, versioned semantic substrate under the AI. Competitors' AI features overwhelmingly prompt over categorized transactions; Fourth Meridian's assembler pipeline feeds *classified flows, resolved transfers, evidence-tagged facts, and deterministic assessments* with a numeric-claim output validator that annotates or blocks fabricated figures. That validator alone is more mature than anything visible in the category. (2) Epistemic honesty as a product primitive — estimated/observed tags, completeness tiers, `UNKNOWN` as an answer, coverage caveats serialized into the prompt. Incumbents assert; this system discloses. It is a durable trust differentiator *if surfaced*. (3) Multi-currency as a first-class design (per-space reporting currency, taint-propagating conversion) — genuinely rare; Kubera aside, US-centric competitors are weak here. (4) An investments event log with reconstruction — Monarch/Copilot hold current positions; Quicken has history but no semantic layer over it.

**Advantages not built.** (1) Everything users actually compare on: budgets/envelopes at competitive depth, bill detection and recurring-payment intelligence, shared-finance UX polish, mobile. (2) Speed of insight: competitors answer instantly from precomputed aggregates; Fourth Meridian recomputes per request and its historical price coverage is near-zero pending a vendor license — the repo audit's "single highest-leverage external unblock." A10 honestly answering "incomplete" for most past dates is correct and commercially underwhelming. (3) Data breadth: Plaid-sandbox default, observed sync depth often 24–99 days, one aggregator. Kubera's asset-class breadth and Quicken's decades of edge cases are moats of accumulation that no ontology substitutes for. (4) Any proof that the semantic advantage converts to retention — no analytics exist to even measure it.

The honest framing: Fourth Meridian has built the *engine* competitors would need three years to retrofit, and has not built the *car* customers test-drive in the first ten minutes.

## 9. Biggest risks

**What would scare me.** (1) The proportion problem: solo maintainer, pre-users, Series-B process weight, 212KB truth file that went 72 commits stale in 48 hours, and an audit that once answered a verification question by citing a ledger that cited the audit. The system of record for the project has a measurable error rate, and correcting drift is now its own initiative class. (2) Float money under a wealth-reconstruction product. (3) The numbers disagreeing: three read patterns, six history mechanisms, and documented same-date divergence between surfaces — for a product whose entire brand is honest numbers, KD-21 (visibility leaks on newer surfaces: "the canonical spine now leaks what `getHoldings` correctly redacts") is the scariest single line in the corpus, because it means the newest code is regressing the oldest guarantees. (4) Registration defaulting `open` in prod behind an unverified-CAPTCHA, no revocation blocklist, one encryption key — the security floor is below the engineering ceiling by a wide margin. (5) `SpaceDashboard.tsx` as the chokepoint every initiative queues on.

**What would excite me.** The pure cores are excellent and extraction-ready; the doctrine oracle (255 table-driven invariant checks) is a real asset; the candor of the internal audits means the team (of one) has none of the usual self-deception — every problem in this review, the project already knows about in some form, which is rare and valuable. The AI guardrail work (deterministic output validation, bounded drilldowns, visibility-gated context) is ahead of the market. And the registry seams mean the platform *wants* to become extensible; it just hasn't been forced to.

**What I would investigate first.** In order: (1) run one account through all six history mechanisms and diff the numbers for the same dates — quantify the disagreement before trusting any surface; (2) the KD-21 visibility leaks end-to-end, because sharing + leaks is the one combination that kills a finance product; (3) whether the Decimal migration is actually executable post-hoc (write the migration plan now, even if deferred); (4) what it costs to activate the dark A-series honestly — flags into env, `asOf` through the HTTP boundary, one wired consumer for `loadInvestmentsSpaceData`; (5) whether Phase 0A (single URL authority) can land in under a week, as the leading indicator of whether the decomposition plan is real.
