## 1. Architecture health

Scores are relative to a solo-built, pre-launch fintech, not to a funded team.

| Dimension | Score |
|---|---|
| Overall architecture | 7/10 |
| Backend organization | 7/10 |
| Frontend organization | 4/10 |
| Domain modeling | 7/10 |
| Separation of concerns | 7/10 |
| Scalability | 5/10 |
| Extensibility | 6/10 |
| Technical debt | 6/10 |
| Product readiness | 3/10 |

**Overall architecture — 7/10.** The expensive-to-retrofit decisions are right and consistently enforced: single auth chokepoint, single LLM provider seam (`lib/ai/provider.ts`), single decrypt module, multi-tenant Space isolation with no admin bypass, HKDF per-purpose key derivation. The deterministic-first AI design — the model narrates pre-computed, provenance-stamped facts and never does math — is genuinely above industry norm and is the product's real moat. Loses points for verification that lives in prose rather than enforced code, and for several migration seams held open simultaneously.

**Backend organization — 7/10.** `lib/` is coherent and domain-sliced (`ai`, `plaid`, `providers`, `perspective-engine`, `events`, `data`, `spaces`, `transactions`). Boundaries are real. Pulled down by a handful of 1,000–2,000-line god-files (`lib/ai/intelligence/annotations.ts` 2,086 lines; `app/api/ai/chat/route.ts` 2,003; `lib/ai/assemblers/transactions.ts` 1,025) and by the domain-event system (EV-1) being one emit file plus a single handler — a seam, not yet a spine.

**Frontend organization — 4/10.** Weakest layer by a wide margin. `components/dashboard/SpaceDashboard.tsx` is 2,529 lines; `ManageSpaceModal.tsx` 1,645; `DashboardClient.tsx` 1,512; `SpacesClient.tsx` 1,208; `DebtClient.tsx` 1,148 — unsplit monoliths. STATUS lists "monolith component decomposition rides along" with v2.5; it hasn't happened. The Atlas design-system work added new primitives on top of the monoliths rather than decomposing them, so complexity rose.

**Domain modeling — 7/10.** Expressive schema (55 models/enums, 1,555 lines). The money-sharing model (`SpaceAccountLink` + `VisibilityLevel` + `AccountConnection` + `FinancialAccount`) is well thought through; `FlowType`/`FlowDirection`/`FlowClassificationReason` already in schema. Persistent smells: money is still `Float`, and legacy `Account` coexists with `FinancialAccount`, so the core entity has two representations.

**Separation of concerns — 7/10.** Visibility enforcement funnels through one predicate (`lib/ai/visibility.ts`) shared by the AI assemblers and the data layer — the right pattern; it closed the KD-1/15/19 leak class. Undercut by the chat route acting as intent router, context serializer, validator caller, and budget guard at once.

**Scalability — 5/10.** Fine for near-zero load. But there is no job substrate: `jobs/scheduler.ts` `startScheduler()` is never invoked, `sync-crypto` and `run-ai-advice` are stubs, and the audit log writes ~2 rows per chat per Space (KD-12). Nothing handles fan-out yet.

**Extensibility — 6/10.** Adding a lens (Perspective Engine) or an assembler is clean — the registries are the best-designed parts of the codebase. Adding a second provider is not: the code is Plaid-shaped throughout despite a `Connection`/`ProviderType` abstraction. ProviderAdapter is correctly parked as speculation until a real second provider exists.

**Technical debt — 6/10.** Lower than the doc volume implies, but real: no test runner, dead `lib/mock-data.ts`, 19 empty `" 2"` ghost directories from cloud-sync, legacy `Account` still read in three runtime paths, and stale STATUS.md.

**Product readiness — 3/10.** No billing, no onboarding funnel, Plaid still in sandbox, no ambient/notification substrate, `AiAdvice` has never had a write path. An architecture demo with one deep vertical (AI analysis of connected accounts), not yet a product a stranger can pay for.

## 2. Roadmap alignment

**On track on the hard axis, drifting on the easy one.** The security/data-integrity spine is ahead of where a solo pre-launch project usually is. The product surface is behind, and the gap is widening because engineering energy keeps flowing to the spine.

**Drifting from vision:** the last ~40 commits are almost entirely infrastructure and chrome — EV-1 event plumbing, SP-2 authorization centralization, Liquid card visuals, Daily Brief backdrop standardization, timeline event emission. None are things a user asked for or would notice as new value. The product hasn't gained a user-facing capability since the AI chat matured. Motion in the engine room, quiet on the deck.

**Over-engineering:** the initiative-naming/aliasing governance (D-number freeze, track-prefix namespace rules), 63 investigation docs and 23 initiative folders for a single maintainer, and the MC1 multi-currency track — a 7-phase "foundational architecture" plan for a USD-only product with zero users and zero non-USD accounts.

**Under-building:** everything that closes the loop for a real user — onboarding, the ambient/notification layer the AI ladder is building toward, and product surfaces for intelligence already computed.

## 3. Recent v2.5 work — was it worth it?

**SP-2 (authorization centralization)** — worth it, correctly scoped. Centralizing Space authz into a policy predicate before building more Space surfaces is the right sequencing. Low regret. Keep. (Not recorded in the STATUS initiative ledger — see drift note.)

**EV-1 (domain event seam)** — worth it, but watch the scope. A typed emit/dispatch seam with transaction-aware ordering is sound. But it's one real handler (snapshot regeneration) and many "audit-only, no handler" event types. Risk: building an event bus in anticipation of ambient intelligence two milestones away. Worth laying the seam; would be over-building to expand it before there's a consumer. Freeze it where it is until v2.6b needs it.

**Timeline Foundation** — over-built relative to its truth. `lib/timeline-placeholder.ts` injects `isPreview: true` demo events (documents, wallets, AI recommendations, investment milestones) for event types that have no producer; only two real producers exist (member invite/join, goal check-ins). The Timeline looks finished but is mostly scaffolding badged as preview. Architecture fine; it presents as complete when ~80% of its vocabulary is placeholder. Scope was too broad — the whole event taxonomy built before the producers.

**Daily Brief** — reasonable, but thrashing. The log shows backdrop standardization, simplification, a tracked-accounts detail tab, a distinct-accounts count fix, plus two investigation docs about the same counter. High iteration on one card's polish and one number. Poor effort-to-value ratio.

**Spaces redesign / Liquid cards** — over-built, and the doc mis-states it. STATUS says Liquid was "evaluated and rejected" and parked with "two npm deps uncommitted pending prune." Reality: `AtlasLiquidCard`/`AtlasLiquidCta` are committed and in active use across `SpacesClient` and four Brief components, and the material was vendored into `components/atlas/vendor/liquid-glass/`. The visual direction shipped. This is the clearest case of engineering attention (custom glass material, palette-ratchet guards, per-depth filter tiers) spent where a user would not notice. Would do differently: stop the material engine at "looks good," build no doctrine around it.

## 4. Codebase health — what's getting messy

**Documentation, first and worst.** STATUS.md declares itself the only current-state authority ("if it conflicts with the code, fix this file") and is 41 commits stale (last verified `37f96f3`, HEAD `2db97ef`). It lists merchant-normalization tests as absent (they exist), describes the working tree as full of uncommitted Liquid experiments (clean but for 4 untracked docs), calls Liquid rejected (it shipped), and never records SP-2 or EV-1 in the ledger. The single-source-of-truth doctrine has already failed its own test. 63 investigation docs + 23 initiative folders is more documentation than one person can keep true.

**No test runner.** 22 `.test.ts` files, each a standalone `tsx` script (no jest, no vitest, no `npm test`, no runner binary installed). No single command runs them, no CI aggregation. STATUS asserts suites are "green" but nothing enforces it and nothing stops a regression. The tests are real and well-written; the discipline around them is absent. Highest-leverage cheap fix in the audit.

**Ghost directories.** 19 empty `" 2"` directories from cloud-sync, now spread into source paths (`app/api/spaces/[id] 2`, `components/space/widgets 2`, `lib/ai/intent 2`). Empty and untracked, so harmless to the build — but noise, and one stray file in the wrong twin would be a nasty bug.

**God-files.** The seven files over 1,000 lines are the real coupling/duplication risk; `chat/route.ts` and `annotations.ts` especially.

**Dead code.** `lib/mock-data.ts` is imported by nothing but itself.

## 5. Hidden technical debt

**Two-representation account model.** Legacy `Account` still coexists with `FinancialAccount` and is still read in `lib/imports/authorize.ts`, `app/api/admin/overview/route.ts`, and `app/api/accounts/[id]/transactions/route.ts`. v2.5's own exit criterion is "zero legacy-`Account` queries in read paths." Not met. Every new read path written against a to-be-retired model makes retirement harder.

**Verification-as-prose.** The most dangerous debt is cultural: invariants asserted in comments and STATUS prose rather than checked in runnable, enforced code. "Tests green" with no runner means the guarantee decays silently.

**Event system as speculative abstraction.** EV-1 has more event types than handlers; until v2.6b most events go to the audit log and nowhere else. Unused abstraction accruing surface area.

**Doc drift as a category.** STATUS being 41 commits stale is structural, not a one-off — the volume of immutable investigation docs guarantees the living doc can't stay ahead.

**`Float` money.** Parked deliberately, but every day of transactions is more rows to migrate to Decimal/int-cents.

**`AiAdvice` with no writer.** A modeled, queried table (`lib/data/advice.ts`) never written to. Reads as a live feature; is a stub.

## 6. Product audit (ignoring code quality)

**What would make it feel dramatically more premium:** the moment between "connect a bank" and "see something valuable." That's the connect → wait → manual-refresh gap D2.x is chartered to fix — correctly identified as flagship, still queued, not built. A first-run that shows balances in seconds and streams history visibly would do more for perceived quality than the entire Atlas material engine. Premium lives in latency and first-run, not in glass.

**Missing capabilities:** anything ambient — the product only speaks when spoken to (`AiAdvice` writer doesn't exist). No notifications, no proactive insight. Onboarding absent. No way to act on an insight (no budgets; goals are check-in only).

**Incomplete journeys:** Timeline (mostly placeholder), Daily Brief (heavily iterated but thin), the advice loop (compute → store → surface → notify is missing its back half).

**Too much engineering, not enough product:** the design-system material engine, the event bus, the multi-currency charter, the documentation-governance apparatus.

## 7. Re-ranked priorities

The current ordering optimizes correctness → conversation → ambient (the "AI evolution ladder"). Clean, but it defers everything a user experiences to v2.6+. Re-weight toward first-run and enforcement.

1. **A test runner + CI gate.** ~Half a day. Converts 22 orphan scripts into an enforced guarantee and de-risks everything below.
2. **D2.x Initial Sync Experience.** Biggest perceived-quality lever; the engine already exists — orchestration, not new infrastructure. Pull ahead of more AI-layer work.
3. **SP-1 / seam closure — finish legacy `Account` retirement out of read paths.** Stop building on two account models.
4. **Data semantics (v2.5.5 / flowType).** Real content (KD-17 sign-doctrine findings), schema already present. Do before advisor work builds on it.
5. **AI-5 advisor coherence.** Real, but polishing a surface most users haven't reached.
6. **Planner / ProviderAdapter / Search / MC1.** Defer. Keep ProviderAdapter and MC1 parked as speculation until a real second provider / non-USD account exists.

Change from the existing plan: first-run experience and enforcement move above the AI conversation ladder.

## 8. Blind spots

**Consistently overlooked:** the user's first five minutes, and the back half of every loop. Three features share one shape — sophisticated front half, missing consumer: `AiAdvice` (no writer), EV-1 (few handlers), Timeline (few producers).

**Too much time on:** governance and abstraction-in-advance — ID-freeze rules, 63 investigation docs, material doctrine, multi-currency charter. Architecture done beautifully, product reluctantly. The documentation is a bureaucracy partly managing complexity the bureaucracy itself encourages.

**Avoided hard work:** shipping something a stranger can use end-to-end (onboarding, billing, production Plaid, the ambient loop). Also deleting — ghost dirs, mock-data, placeholder timeline, vendored-then-"rejected" Liquid all persist because pruning is less satisfying than building.

**Painful in v3:** `Float` money (grows daily); the two-account-model coexistence (retire now at 3 files, not later at 30); verification-as-prose (the day a KD-defect regresses silently, the "we're disciplined about correctness" thesis breaks).

## 9. If I had one month

**One thing: ship a real end-to-end first-run, gated by real tests.** Week one: stand up a test runner and CI gate. Weeks two–four: build D2.x so a new user connects a bank, sees balances in seconds, watches history stream in, and lands on a Daily Brief and AI answer that are actually populated — no manual refresh, no empty placeholder timeline.

Why only this: it simultaneously closes the widening perception gap, forces the missing enforcement discipline, exercises the sync engine / snapshot backfill / AI layer end-to-end, and is the prerequisite for every growth path. Smarter analyst (AI-5), cleaner data (v2.5.5), shinier glass (UI-1) all assume a user who got far enough to care. Fix the front door before redecorating rooms.

## 10. What should NOT be touched

Done enough — stop revisiting:

- **Security spine** — auth chokepoint, HKDF derivation, session revocation, TOTP/recovery, SYSTEM_ADMIN separation. KD-6 re-encryption is the only residual (and is effectively resolved — see drift note). Don't refactor.
- **Tenancy + visibility model** — `SpaceAccountLink` / `VisibilityLevel` / shared `lib/ai/visibility.ts`. Leak class closed, proven by the two-user test. Stop poking it.
- **Deterministic AI pipeline architecture** — context-builder, assembler/signal registries, "model narrates, never calculates," output validator. Best work in the codebase. Extend with new assemblers/lenses; don't re-architect.
- **Atlas glass material engine.** Looks good. Stop polishing, stop writing doctrine, prune the unused vendored Liquid code.
- **Initiative-governance apparatus.** Past positive return. Add no more namespace rules; collapse docs if anything.

## 11. Final verdict

**Easier or harder to evolve?** Slightly harder than three months ago, and the trend is the concern. The spine got stronger but the surface (seven 1,000+ line components) got bigger without decomposition, the doc corpus grew past maintainability, and STATUS.md — the thing meant to make evolution safe — is stale. Core more evolvable; edges less.

**Architecture improving?** In depth, yes. In breadth, it's accreting speculative abstraction (event bus, MC1 charter, material doctrine) and unenforced guarantees.

**Right product?** Right engine, wrong amount of it before shipping. Over-invested in correctness and chrome, under-invested in the first-run loop that makes both matter.

**Biggest missed opportunity:** the first five minutes. D2.x is chartered, understood, and not built.

**Single highest-leverage initiative after v2.5:** a test runner with a CI gate, immediately followed by D2.x. The runner converts prose guarantees into enforced ones for near-zero cost; D2.x converts a hidden engine into a felt experience. Everything else waits behind those two.

The blunt version: an unusually disciplined engine, an unusually elaborate bureaucracy describing it, and less energy than assumed on anything a user can touch. The fix isn't more architecture — it's enforcement, deletion, and the front door.
